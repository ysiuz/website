That's my Voice

Visual Identity

AI voices are everywhere — used to streamline, manipulate, entertain and indulge. Yet, is there more to these vocal mimicries than just the prompts we give them?
Across the surfaces of 20 Eamses cards, I explore the nuance in all of our voices.
It should be noted though, that my work is only made possible through the crucial reporting done about AI voices by Max Read of the New York Times, Allegra Rosenberg of Business Insider, and Pranshu Verma of the Washington Post [ 1, 2 ].
Their work serves as the structure and inspiration for everything that you see below.

Time:

5 weeks

Role:

Sole Designer

Tools:

Ai, Ps, Digital Fabrication (laser cutting :>).

In Context

Things sound complicated — but they aren't. I designed a series of cards, their delivery method, and a system of construction for people to loosely follow.
And here's all the card's put together on my desk. I really love the way some sides of the tower feel cohesive and gridded, whereas other sides do not. It feels like an accurate represenation of a messy choir of voices of speaking and singing at the same time. Speaking of messy, please ingore my desk :O

Voice Through Type

A human voice just feels different than an artificial one.
To visually highlight this dissonance in sound, my human and AI typography behave in unique ways, and respond differently to the grid I built.
Human voices are generally free, able to emphasize, pause, and stutter. In reponse, human type feels like it should largely behave according to the context and emotion of the speaker, and not the grid around it. With this same contextual nuance in mind, Exposure, a variable typface which can bleed and shrink, felt right.
In contrast, AI voices feel manipulated and awkward, lacking contextual awareness. To reflect this, my AI typography lacks agency and adheres strictly to the grid. Moreoever, I chose Gravity, as its typeface, given the weird strength it projects - like a kid who can't control their volume. Of course, Exposure is present behind any instance of Gravity, as I wanted to maintain the sense of "manipulated humaness" within my AI type.

Colorful Sound

Since my type system has a lot of complexity, my color relationships are relatively straightforward.
AI is digital and cold, so greens, blues, and teals work.
On the other hand, humans are passionate and warm so they use pinks, purples, maroons, etc.
This warm-cool relationship often creates sharp contrast between voices within my cards, but also moments of confusion and surprising nuance when the colors mix.

Lyrical Navigation

To hint at an advised route of interacting with the cards, I wanted to guide people using famous lyrics. Every card has 2 incomplete lyrics on their corners, which are then completed by slotting other cards in. Therefore, to progress my intended narrative you have to challenge yourself to be sensitive to every voice on a card, including these disembodied lyrics.

The Box

To package all my cards together, I lasercut a simple box with a slot. Since the cards themselves inform their own construction using their slots and my navigation, I chose to use the packaging to highlight the grid, and specific voices within it. What exactly is highlighted in those boxes as a person pulls a card out, is entirely random. I think it's fun.
In this case: He Ho. Or I guess Ho He :>

Reflection

This project was definitely the most challenging thing I've taken on scale-wise. 20 cards, 40 surfaces is no joke. Though my subject matter of voice initially complicated my approach, my grid system ended up making the project less conceptually difficult and more simply a trial of patience and time. I was willing to fill those boxes in, and so things worked out.
While succesful, this approach came at the cost of time which I could have spent refining my interaction with the cards. I didn't get the chance to test and experiment with how people explore my cards very much, which is unfortunate. Well, you win some and you lose some :D
